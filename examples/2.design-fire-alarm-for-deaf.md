# Example: Design a fire alarm for the deaf

> **Prompt:** "Design a fire alarm for the deaf."

---

## Section 1 — Clarify (~2 min)

Before I dive in, I'd like to ask a couple of clarifying questions to make sure I'm solving the right problem.

- **Are we designing a consumer product for homes, or an institutional system for offices, hotels, and public buildings?** I'll assume we're designing a consumer product for home use, since that's where deaf individuals have the least institutional support and the most personal risk — especially while sleeping.
- **Since no specific company was mentioned, I'll assume we're a startup exploring this space.** I'll assume we're a hardware-capable startup focused on assistive technology, with the ability to ship a physical device paired with a mobile app.

I'll proceed with those assumptions — let me know if you'd like me to adjust.

---

## Section 2 — Rationale (~5 min)

Now let me walk through my rationale for why this is worth building.

**Market:**
- **Big market:** The deaf and hard-of-hearing population is substantial globally, and aging populations are driving growth in hearing loss. Meanwhile, smart home adoption is accelerating, creating new infrastructure for connected safety devices.
- **Why important?** Fire is life-threatening, and the inability to hear a standard smoke alarm is a direct safety gap — it affects not just the individual but their families and dependents.
- **Why now?** Smart home platforms have matured enough that a connected alert device can integrate with existing ecosystems at low cost, and regulatory attention on accessibility in housing is increasing.

**Company Fit:**
- **Competitive landscape:** Existing solutions fall into three categories: strobe-light alarms (Kidde, Gentex), bed-shaker devices (Sonic Alert, Bellman & Symfon), and general smart home smoke detectors (Nest Protect, First Alert).
- **Market gap:** Strobe and shaker devices are single-modality — they work in one room, require separate installation, and don't adapt to the user's context (asleep vs. awake, home vs. away). Smart detectors like Nest Protect are designed for hearing users and bolt on accessibility as an afterthought.
- **Unique strength:** As a focused assistive-tech startup, we can design the entire experience around deaf users from day one — not retrofit accessibility onto a hearing-first product.

**1-Line Thesis:** Existing fire alarms treat deaf users as an edge case, leaving a dangerous gap between detection and action that a purpose-built, multi-sensory alert system can close.

---

## Section 3 — Product Goal (~2 min)

With that rationale in mind, here's the product goal I'd orient the team around.

**Help deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals receive and act on fire alerts instantly, so that hearing loss is never a factor in home fire safety.**

Success looks like: a deaf person asleep at 3 a.m. wakes up within seconds of smoke detection, understands the situation immediately, and exits safely — with the same confidence a hearing person has.

---

## Section 4 — Segmentation (~5 min)

Next, I'll segment the market. Before diving in, I want to lay out my prioritization logic so we can evaluate as we go. I'll prioritize based on: Reach, Impact, and Strategic Fit with the product goal and company strengths.

**Ecosystem Players:**
- Deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals (end-user)
- Family members and roommates of deaf individuals (support role)
- Audiologists and deaf community organizations (intermediary)
- Property managers and landlords (enabler — control what gets installed)
- Fire safety regulators and ADA compliance bodies (enabler — set standards and mandates)

I'd focus on deaf and hard-of-hearing individuals. **Reach: high** — they are the primary audience whose safety gap we're solving. **Impact: high** — the product directly changes their survival odds in a fire. **Strategic fit: high** — our entire thesis is built around serving them first.

**Primary Dimension — Degree of Hearing Loss:** This changes the product need fundamentally — someone with partial hearing may respond to amplified sound, while a profoundly deaf person needs entirely non-auditory modalities.
- Mild to moderate hearing loss
- Severe hearing loss
- Profoundly deaf / no residual hearing

I'd prioritize profoundly deaf users. **Reach: medium** — smaller than the full hearing-loss population, but still a large and well-defined group. **Impact: high** — they have zero benefit from traditional alarms, so the safety gap is most extreme. **Strategic fit: high** — designing for the hardest case forces us to build a truly multi-sensory product that works for everyone down the stack.

**Secondary Dimension — Living Situation:**
- Lives alone
- Lives with hearing family or roommates
- Lives in assisted or institutional housing

I'd prioritize those who live alone. **Reach: medium** — a significant portion of deaf adults live independently. **Impact: high** — there's no one else to wake them or relay the alarm. **Strategic fit: high** — this is the scenario where the product is literally life-or-death, which sharpens our MVP scope.

> So my target segment is profoundly deaf individuals who live alone.

**Persona:** Marcus is a 42-year-old profoundly deaf software developer who lives alone in a one-bedroom apartment and relies entirely on visual and haptic cues for all alerts. He cares about living independently without compromising his safety.

---

## Section 5 — Pain Points (~10 min)

**Awareness & Detection:**
- **Standard alarms are invisible to him** — his building's smoke detectors are audio-only and he has no idea if they've ever gone off while he was home
- **No confidence the system works** — he's never tested whether his current setup would actually wake him, and has no way to verify

**Alerting While Asleep:**
- **Strobe lights don't wake deep sleepers** — his bedroom door is closed, and a hallway strobe won't penetrate; even bedside strobes don't reliably wake heavy sleepers
- **Bed shakers are single-room and clunky** — they require a separate device under the mattress, only work in the bedroom, and feel like a medical device from the 1990s
- **No escalation if the first alert fails** — if he doesn't wake from one stimulus, nothing ramps up or tries a different modality

**Understanding the Situation:**
- **Alert doesn't communicate what's happening** — even if he wakes up, he doesn't know if it's smoke, CO, a false alarm, or a neighbor's alarm bleeding through the wall
- **No location information** — in a multi-room home, he can't tell where the danger is

**Taking Action:**
- **Can't call 911 effectively in a panic** — TTY and relay services add critical delay; texting 911 isn't universally available
- **No way to alert neighbors or building** — he can't shout for help, and there's no automated way to notify others he's in danger
- **No post-event record** — after a false alarm or real event, he has no log to review what happened and when

I'll prioritize pain points by two dimensions: frequency — how often users encounter this — and severity — how much it blocks the job to be done, how underserved it is by existing solutions, and the emotional toll it takes.

The top pain point is **strobe lights don't wake deep sleepers.**

**Frequency:** This is relevant every single night — any night he's asleep is a night when an audio-only or strobe-only alarm may fail to wake him. It's the most consistently present risk.

**Severity:** It directly blocks the job to be done — if he doesn't wake up, nothing else matters. This is deeply underserved; bed shakers exist but are unreliable, single-room, and rarely integrated with modern smoke detectors. The emotional toll is a quiet, persistent dread — he goes to bed knowing that if a fire starts tonight, he might not wake up.

---

## Section 6 — Solution (~15 min)

- **Multi-Sensory Wake System:** A bedside device that combines intense haptic vibration (pillow or wristband), strobe light, and optional scent release to wake the user through redundant non-auditory channels.
- **Smart Alert Hub:** A connected hub that links to existing smoke and CO detectors and pushes contextual alerts (what, where, severity) to the user's phone, smartwatch, and smart home devices.
- **Emergency Auto-Dispatch:** An automated system that contacts 911 via text-to-911 or relay, shares the user's address and situation, and sends alerts to pre-set emergency contacts and neighbors.

To evaluate these, I'll use two criteria: User Impact — how well it solves the problem and how desirable it is — and Effort — the complexity and time to build.

- **Multi-Sensory Wake System:** **High user impact** — directly solves the #1 pain of not waking up, with redundant channels to ensure reliability. **High effort** — requires custom hardware design, haptic engineering, and safety certification.
- **Smart Alert Hub:** **Medium user impact** — valuable for context and connected alerting, but doesn't solve the core wake-up problem alone. **Medium effort** — software-heavy with hardware integrations, leverages existing detector infrastructure.
- **Emergency Auto-Dispatch:** **Medium user impact** — critical in a real event but only matters if the user is already awake. **Low effort** — primarily software, leveraging existing APIs and messaging protocols.

**MVP: Multi-Sensory Wake System**

I'd build the Multi-Sensory Wake System as our v1. Core features:
- Wristband with escalating haptic vibration — starts gentle, ramps to aggressive over 10 seconds
- Pairs with any standard smoke detector via sound-listening base station (microphone detects alarm tone)
- Companion app confirms connection status and lets the user test the system weekly
- Bright bedside strobe activates simultaneously as a secondary channel

**v1 exclusions:** No contextual alert information (what room, what type of hazard — save for v2 Smart Alert Hub). No automated 911 dispatch (save for v2 — requires regulatory work and text-to-911 coverage mapping).

**Risks + Mitigations:**
- **Risk:** Haptic wristband battery dies overnight. **Mitigation:** Design for 7-day battery life with low-battery push alerts to the user's phone, and make the bedside strobe function independently of the wristband.
- **Risk:** Sound-listening base station triggers false alarms from TV or cooking smoke. **Mitigation:** Train the audio model specifically on certified alarm tones (T3 pattern) and add a 10-second confirmation window before full alert escalation.

To summarize: I'd focus on profoundly deaf individuals who live alone, specifically around the pain of strobe lights failing to wake deep sleepers, and build a Multi-Sensory Wake System as our first bet. Happy to go deeper on any section.
