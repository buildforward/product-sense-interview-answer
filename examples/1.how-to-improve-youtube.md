# Example: How would you improve YouTube?

> **Prompt:** "How would you improve YouTube?"

---

## Section 1 — Clarify (~2 min)

Before I dive in, I'd like to ask a couple of clarifying questions to make sure I'm solving the right problem.

- **Are we improving the core YouTube platform, or a specific surface like YouTube Shorts, YouTube Music, or YouTube TV?** I'll assume we're focused on the core YouTube experience — the main app and website where users discover, watch, and engage with long-form and mid-form video content.
- **Are we optimizing for a specific user type — viewers, creators, or advertisers?** I'll assume we'll consider the full ecosystem but ultimately prioritize one player based on where the biggest opportunity lies.

I'll proceed with those assumptions — let me know if you'd like me to adjust.

---

## Section 2 — Rationale (~5 min)

Now let me walk through my rationale for why this is worth building.

**Market:**
- **Big market:** Online video is one of the largest consumer attention categories globally, and watch time continues to shift from linear TV to on-demand platforms. YouTube is the dominant player, but competition for viewer time is intensifying across TikTok, Netflix, and emerging AI-native content platforms.
- **Why important?** Video is how people learn, entertain themselves, and connect with culture — it's central to daily life for billions of users.
- **Why now?** The rise of short-form video has fragmented attention, creator burnout is accelerating, and AI-generated content is flooding the platform — all of which threaten content quality and viewer trust.

**Company Fit:**
- **Mission fit:** Google's mission is to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible. YouTube extends this to video — improving how people find and engage with the right content is core to the mission.
- **Business objective:** YouTube's primary revenue driver is ad-supported watch time. Improvements that increase session depth, return frequency, or creator output directly impact revenue. YouTube Premium and channel memberships are secondary but growing.
- **Competitive landscape:** The competitive set includes short-form platforms (TikTok, Instagram Reels), streaming services (Netflix, Disney+), and emerging AI/social video (Runway, AI-generated content feeds).
- **Market gap:** TikTok dominates discovery-driven short-form. Netflix owns lean-back long-form. YouTube sits uniquely in the middle but underinvests in intentional viewing — users often feel stuck in recommendation loops rather than finding what they actually came for.
- **Unique strength:** YouTube's creator ecosystem is unmatched in scale and diversity — no other platform has the breadth of educational, entertainment, and niche content across virtually every topic and language.

**1-Line Thesis:** YouTube's greatest risk isn't losing creators or viewers to competitors — it's that the recommendation experience optimizes for watch time at the expense of viewer satisfaction, which erodes long-term trust and retention.

---

## Section 3 — Product Goal (~2 min)

With that rationale in mind, here's the product goal I'd orient the team around.

**Help YouTube viewers consistently find content they're glad they watched, so that YouTube becomes the platform people choose intentionally — not just default to.**

Success looks like: a viewer finishes a session feeling like their time was well spent, returns the next day by choice, and can articulate why YouTube is valuable to them — not just habitual.

---

## Section 4 — Segmentation (~5 min)

Next, I'll segment the market. Before diving in, I want to lay out my prioritization logic so we can evaluate as we go. I'll prioritize based on: Reach, Impact, and Strategic Fit with the product goal and company strengths.

**Ecosystem Players:**
- Viewers (end-user — consume content)
- Creators (supply-side — produce and upload content)
- Advertisers (demand-side — fund the ecosystem through ad spend)
- Multi-channel networks and talent managers (intermediary — represent and monetize creators)
- Moderators and trust & safety teams (support role — maintain platform quality)

I'd focus on viewers. **Reach: high** — billions of monthly active users, the largest player group by orders of magnitude. **Impact: high** — improving their experience directly drives the satisfaction metric at the core of our product goal. **Strategic fit: high** — viewer satisfaction is the upstream lever that drives watch time, ad revenue, and creator incentive.

**Primary Dimension — Viewing Intent:** Intent fundamentally changes what "improvement" means — a learner needs different discovery, UI, and feedback than someone killing time. Different intents require different product solutions.
- Goal-directed learning (tutorials, how-tos, courses)
- Entertainment browsing (comedy, music, vlogs, trending)
- Deep-dive research (long-form essays, documentaries, analysis)

I'd prioritize goal-directed learners. **Reach: high** — educational content is one of YouTube's largest categories, spanning students, professionals, hobbyists, and DIYers. **Impact: high** — these users have the sharpest intent-satisfaction gap; they come with a specific goal and the current experience often derails them into unrelated recommendations. **Strategic fit: high** — this is YouTube's deepest moat versus TikTok and Netflix, neither of which serves structured learning well.

**Secondary Dimension — Expertise Level:**
- Beginners (exploring a new topic, need orientation)
- Intermediate (know the basics, need depth and nuance)
- Advanced (deep practitioners, need cutting-edge or niche content)

I'd prioritize beginners. **Reach: high** — the largest group by volume; everyone starts as a beginner in new domains. **Impact: high** — beginners are most vulnerable to low-quality or misleading content and most likely to abandon YouTube when they can't find a clear learning path. **Strategic fit: high** — serving beginners well creates long-term users who graduate into intermediate and advanced viewers on the platform.

> So my target segment is beginner goal-directed learners.

**Persona:** Priya is a 28-year-old marketing coordinator who turns to YouTube when she needs to learn a new skill for work — like SQL, Figma, or presentation design — and has limited time to find the right starting point. She cares about finding trustworthy, well-structured content quickly without getting sidetracked.

---

## Section 5 — Pain Points (~10 min)

**Discovering What to Learn:**
- **No clear starting point** — she searches "learn SQL" and gets a wall of thumbnails with no signal on which is beginner-appropriate, current, or well-structured
- **Quality is invisible upfront** — view count and likes don't tell her if a tutorial is accurate, well-paced, or complete

**Selecting the Right Content:**
- **Overwhelmed by choice** — dozens of seemingly identical "SQL for beginners" videos, no way to compare scope, teaching style, or depth without clicking into each
- **Outdated content ranks high** — a 2018 tutorial with millions of views outranks a better 2024 one because the algorithm favors historical engagement
- **Clickbait titles mismatch actual content** — "Learn SQL in 10 Minutes" turns out to be a 45-minute video that doesn't cover what she needs

**Learning During the Session:**
- **No structured progression** — she finishes one good video but the next recommendation is unrelated; there's no guided path from "intro to SQL" to "SQL joins" to "practice exercises"
- **Recommendations pull her off-task** — sidebar suggestions optimize for engagement, not her learning goal; she came to learn SQL and ends up watching a productivity vlog
- **Can't find the key moment** — she needs to rewatch the section on WHERE clauses but has to scrub through a 30-minute video to find it

**After the Session:**
- **No record of progress** — she can't see what she's covered, what's next, or pick up where she left off across sessions
- **No way to assess what she actually learned** — she watched three videos but has no signal on whether she's ready to apply the skill

I'll prioritize pain points by two dimensions: frequency — how often users encounter this — and severity — how much it blocks the job to be done, how underserved it is by existing solutions, and the emotional toll it takes.

The top pain point is **no structured progression.**

**Frequency:** This hits every single learning session — every time she finishes a video, she faces the same question: "what do I watch next?" It's the most consistently recurring friction in the learning journey.

**Severity:** It directly blocks the job to be done — learning a skill requires sequential building, and without a guided path, she either watches redundant content or skips critical foundations. This is deeply underserved; YouTube has playlists, but they're creator-curated, inconsistent, and rarely designed as a coherent curriculum. The emotional toll is discouragement — she feels like she's wasting time and starts considering paid alternatives like Coursera or Udemy instead.

---

## Section 6 — Solution (~15 min)

- **Learning Paths:** Auto-generated, topic-specific sequences that order the best beginner videos into a structured curriculum with clear progression from foundational to applied.
- **Intent-Aware Recommendations:** A learning mode that locks sidebar and up-next recommendations to the user's stated learning goal for the duration of a session.
- **Key Moments Index:** AI-generated chapter markers and concept labels within videos, so users can jump directly to the section they need and skip what they already know.

To evaluate these, I'll use two criteria: User Impact — how well it solves the problem and how desirable it is — and Effort — the complexity and time to build.

- **Learning Paths:** **High user impact** — directly solves the #1 pain of no structured progression and transforms YouTube from a video library into a learning platform. **High effort** — requires topic modeling, quality ranking, sequence optimization, and a new UI surface.
- **Intent-Aware Recommendations:** **Medium user impact** — reduces distraction but doesn't create structure; it's defensive rather than additive. **Medium effort** — requires a session-level intent signal and modifications to the recommendation model, but builds on existing infra.
- **Key Moments Index:** **Medium user impact** — valuable for efficiency but addresses an in-video problem, not the cross-video progression gap. **Medium effort** — YouTube already has chapters; this extends them with AI-generated labels and concept tagging.

**MVP: Learning Paths**

I'd build Learning Paths as our v1. Core features:
- User searches a learning topic (e.g., "SQL for beginners") and sees a curated path of 5–8 videos, sequenced from intro to applied
- Paths are auto-generated using topic modeling, video quality signals (completion rate, like-to-view ratio, recency), and transcript analysis for content coverage
- Progress bar tracks which videos in the path the user has completed
- "Continue learning" entry point on the homepage for returning users

**v1 exclusions:** No quizzes or knowledge checks (requires content generation beyond video curation — save for v2). No creator-facing tools for submitting or editing paths (start with fully algorithmic generation, add creator input later).

**Risks + Mitigations:**
- **Risk:** Auto-generated paths may sequence videos poorly — e.g., skipping a foundational concept or including redundant content. **Mitigation:** Launch with a small set of high-confidence topics (top 20 searched learning queries) and use human review to validate path quality before scaling.
- **Risk:** Creators whose videos aren't selected for paths may feel disadvantaged, creating ecosystem tension. **Mitigation:** Show transparent criteria for path inclusion and give creators a dashboard showing which paths feature their content, incentivizing quality over clickbait.

To summarize: I'd focus on beginner goal-directed learners, specifically around the pain of no structured progression across videos, and build Learning Paths as our first bet. Happy to go deeper on any section.
